The Problem

Maps optimize for time.
People optimize for safety.

Every navigation app sends you down the fastest path — even if that means a dark underpass, a street with no sidewalk, or a block with a history of incidents. For anyone walking alone, at night, or in an unfamiliar area, "fastest" and "safest" are rarely the same route.

🌙
Walking after dark
Lighting and visibility change everything — but standard maps ignore them entirely.
🚷
Missing sidewalks
The quickest line often routes pedestrians onto roads built only for cars.
📍
Blind to local crime
Routing engines have no idea which blocks people actually avoid.
The Solution

Three routes. One honest answer.

SafePath scores all 686,000 street segments, then runs three weighted shortest-path searches so you can choose your own trade-off between speed and safety.

🛡️

Safest

Aggressively avoids high-crime, poorly-lit, low-walkability segments. Adds a heavy penalty to risky streets so the path routes around them.

⚖️

Balanced

A middle ground — accepts a slightly longer walk to skip the worst blocks, without the full detour of the safest route.

⚡

Fastest

The classic shortest path by distance — shown for comparison so you can see exactly what safety costs you in time.

The Safety Score

What makes a street "safe"?

Every segment gets a 0–1 score blended from three signals. The weights shift automatically between day and night — because lighting matters more after dark.

🚨 Crime SDPD calls for service
50%
🚶 Walkability EPA Smart Location Index
25%
💡 Infrastructure lighting + road type
25%
safety score = 0.50·crime + 0.25·walk + 0.25·infrastructure
The Data Behind It

Real public data, one score

Each signal comes from a real, public source — here's what each one is and why it earns its weight in the safety score.

🚨
Crime
50% day · 45% night
SDPD Calls for Service · 3 years

Three full years of San Diego Police Department calls for service, each geocoded to a point on the map. We score every street by how few incidents happened nearby — separately for day and night.

Why it matters: three years is enough history to tell a recurring pattern from a one-off, so a street only reads "risky" if incidents keep happening there. It's the most direct measure of where people feel unsafe — the heaviest weight, and heavier still at night.
🚶
Walkability
25%
EPA Walkability Index + Census TIGER

An area-level measure from two sources: the U.S. Walkability Index (EPA Smart Location Database) for street connectivity, transit access, and land-use mix — joined to Census TIGER block-group boundaries (California, 2020) so each score maps to a real piece of ground.

Why it matters — the big picture: this is about the whole neighborhood around you, not one street. A high score means a connected, mixed-use area where other people are out walking too; a low score is car-only sprawl where you'd be on your own. It answers "will this area feel alive or deserted?"
💡
Infrastructure
25% day · 30% night
City streetlights + OSMnx road type

A street-level measure combining San Diego's 55,000+ active streetlights with the road type of each segment, read from the OpenStreetMap network via OSMnx — a quiet residential street scores higher than a wide, fast arterial.

Why it matters — the exact street: this zooms in on the specific block under your feet. Two streets in the same neighborhood can be very different — one lit and quiet, the next dark and lined with fast traffic. It answers "is this particular street comfortable to walk right now?" and weighs more at night, when lighting counts most.

Directions
Under the hood

From raw city data to a route in seconds

Real public data gets cleaned, scored onto every street, and pre-computed so the app can find your route almost instantly.

01

Collect

Ingest 4 public sources — 3 yrs of SDPD calls, the EPA Walkability Index, city streetlight points, and the San Diego walking graph pulled from OpenStreetMap via OSMnx.

02

Clean

Geocode call addresses to lat/long, reproject every layer to a common CRS, then spatially join each feature onto its nearest OSM edge.

03

Score

Compute crime, walkability & infrastructure scores for all 686k edges, then blend them into one weighted safety score per edge — separate day and night profiles.

04

Pre-compute

Bake the scored graph into numpy arrays + a SciPy sparse matrix — six cost layers (3 modes × day/night) serialized once so cold start skips all parsing.

05

Route

On each request, geocode the endpoints, snap to the nearest nodes, and run Dijkstra over the sparse cost matrix — sub-second shortest path.

⚡ Pre-computing the graph cut cold start from ~30s (parsing 300 MB of GraphML) to ~2.7s (memory-mapped numpy).
